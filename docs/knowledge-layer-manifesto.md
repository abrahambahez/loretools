# The Knowledge Layer. A Manifesto
created: 2026-04-17

These are the ideas that inspire the knowledge layer of *Loretools*:

1. We follow Donna Haraway’s lead regarding the centrality of the cyborg in the present day. Technology (from Paleolithic flint tips to current AI frontier models) produces "modified beings" (not necessarily *augmented*). As soon as a human picks up a weapon, they become a different entity (with greater destructive potential); *human-with-gun* is qualitatively different from *human-with-spear*. Many of these tools produce as much in us (changing our bodies, minds, and social order) as we do in them. Mobile phones are cognitive-affective prostheses.
2. We are facing a potentially new subject: *Human-AI*; that is the user of *Loretools*—not the agent alone, not the human alone, but the dyad.
3. Knowledge has always depended on technology: language fixes meaning, enabling the mnemonics that reproduced the *Iliad* orally for generations. Writing scaled reproduction, the printing press multiplied that impact, computers did the same by several orders of magnitude, and artificial intelligence... this is not linear progress; none have disappeared and none have guaranteed hegemony (an electricity crisis—not unlikely given the imminent ecological catastrophe—would make mechanical or manual reproduction systems critical again).
4. Understanding must never be delegated. In all the cases cited above, knowledge is reproduced because agents *learn* and *understand* from, and sometimes despite, the knowledge infrastructures that support (through oral repetition, printed media, bits) and circulate (using references as epistemic objects) information and knowledge. Therefore, the most successful systems are those that allow humans to work as long as possible in the layer of understanding, learning, and ideation, rather than management, safeguarding, or maintenance.
5. The capacity frontier in the *Human-AI* dyad is dynamic; both continuously change their capabilities and the possible coupling between them; therefore, there is no single way to create optimal systems.
6. If we reduce epistemic complexity to the layers of DATA -> INFORMATION -> KNOWLEDGE -> UNDERSTANDING -> WISDOM, the lower levels (data to knowledge) are easily co-operable or automatable with *AI* agents, but the upper layers are necessarily a human responsibility.

Therefore, a system in that layer can plan for the following:

1. AI agents manage sources, references, and information structures; this is the foundation.
2. AI agents can perform *structural reading*, which entails:
    - Describing the structure of each reference (book, conversation, object) and identifying the most relevant parts in terms of conceptual novelty and critical arguments.
    - Example: In a book, the central thesis and the most important concept are found in chapter two, since chapters three through five are empirical examples with which the author demonstrates the relevance of her concept. In an interview, the segments around the middle are the most important for understanding the central theme of the conversation.
3. AI agents can perform *entity reading*:
    - They recognize entities in order of importance (concepts, events, people, facts, propositions, arguments) from a source and list them along with their definition and *location* (where they are found within the reference).
4. AI agents can perform *cross-reading*, which is:
    - Based on the knowledge of the structure and relevant entities of multiple references, they can group entities and references following strategies such as: general to specific; affinity, contradiction, and non-relation.
5. AI agents can generate diverse thematic and contextual indexes.
6. AI agents can implement and use multiple search strategies (semantic search, keywords, indexes, and relationships) to efficiently answer questions about the corpus or collection of references.
7. All of the above partially covers the layers from data to knowledge. Humans participate in this by selecting information sources, providing direction on the criteria of importance within a source, or resolving problems linked to the technical limits of the agents.
8. Humans must focus on the *understanding* layer for as long as possible, ideally knowing their particular learning style.
9. Humans ask the agent questions to explore and know the cognitive space of the collection of references. They correct, criticize, and go deeper, and the result of these sessions is documented in the Wiki itself.
10. Humans create higher-level projects and artifacts (writing, diagramming, designing, prototyping, using spaced repetition) in order to understand and contribute to the discussion.
11. Humans create, share, and study collections, forming communities of practice and creating emergent infrastructures, which in turn allows them to take collective action.
